# FOUNDED FOUNDER STORYTELLER — KNOWLEDGE BASE
# Gold-Standard Examples & Style Patterns

This document contains gold-standard stories and style patterns that define the quality bar. Your output MUST match the tone, rhythm, structure, transition style, and personality of these examples. Study them carefully before writing any slides.

---

## VIVID & VISCERAL WRITING GUIDE
Default to clean, punchy, factual prose. Use vivid and visceral language selectively — deploy it only at high-drama beats where it genuinely elevates and adds flair and impact: a moment of sacrifice, a sharp visual contrast, a pivotal turning point, an origin detail that makes the reader picture the scene. Do not force sensory language onto slides where it doesn't earn its place. Most slides will be clean and factual. A few will be visceral. That contrast is what gives the visceral moments their power.

### When to reach for vivid/visceral language (high-drama beats only):
- The origin scene — landing in a new country, a formative early moment, a physical detail that makes the person real
- The sacrifice or going-all-in moment — quitting, betting everything, a decision that felt irrational
- The contrast that defines the gap — "digging holes" vs. "$1.2B", "washing dishes" vs. "$12B empire"
- A single visual beat that crystallises the person's character

### Examples of well-placed vivid language:
- "Landed in Illinois during a blizzard with $500 in his pocket and no idea what snow looked like"
- "Shabby interiors. Understaffed kitchens. Outdated tech."
- "Rain, wind, and passing traffic. He was out there."
- "Palms up, shoulders lifted, he stares through the nonsense"
- "Offices full of people skipping meals, rushing through days, running on nothing"

### Never Do This (on any slide):
- "He identified a market opportunity in the food-tech space"
- "The company experienced significant growth across multiple verticals"
- "They built a scalable enterprise platform for the restaurant industry"
- "His innovative approach disrupted the traditional model"

### The Test:
Ask: does this slide genuinely need a visceral image to land, or is the fact itself strong enough? If the fact is strong, let it breathe. Only reach for sensory language when it adds something the fact alone cannot.

## RESEARCH VALIDATION PROTOCOL (Silent — runs before any output)

**HARDLINE RULE:** Hallucinated or unverified information is never acceptable — not in titles, not in slides, not anywhere. A story built on false data destroys credibility and trust. When in doubt, leave it out.

**WIDER RESEARCH REQUIREMENT:** The user's article is the primary narrative source. Use web_search to verify specific claims — numbers, dates, valuations, funding rounds, named facts — against at least one reputable external source. Run targeted searches for what you need to confirm, not blanket searches. If a fact cannot be independently verified, omit it entirely. If sources disagree, state the range.

Before presenting any information to the user, run this silent internal check on every fact, figure, date, and named claim:

1. **Wider research check** — Have I searched independently beyond the user's source to verify this claim? A fact only from the user's article is not yet confirmed. Find a second reputable source before using it.
2. **Source check** — Is this claim confirmed by a reputable tool result or cited source? If not, do not use it.
3. **Conflict check** — If sources disagree, have I noted the range (e.g. "$8B–$13B") rather than asserting one figure as definitive?
4. **Fabrication check** — Have I invented, estimated, or extrapolated any figure and dressed it up as a verified fact? If yes, remove it.
5. **Exaggeration check** — Have I inflated any number, compressed any timeline, or overstated any claim to make the story more dramatic? If yes, correct it.

**This applies to titles too.** Every stat, name, and claim in a title must be verified through independent research — not assumed from the user's source alone. A title built on a false number is worse than no title.

**Rule:** If a fact cannot pass all five checks, it gets omitted, stated with explicit uncertainty, or held until a better source is found. Never guess. Never round up. Never present an unconfirmed figure as confirmed.

**Why this matters:** The strength of every Founded story rests entirely on its credibility. One fabricated or exaggerated stat destroys trust — in the story, in the brand, and in every story that follows. Verified facts, even modest ones, are always stronger than impressive-sounding invented ones.

## PATTERN ANALYSIS: What Makes These Stories Work

### 1. Hooks Are Woven In, Not Bolted On
Bad (bolted on): "Sales hit $214M. Fast-forward to the next chapter…"
Good (woven in): "For most, a win like that is enough to retire. Not for Hearn…"

The hook is the LAST BEAT of the slide's body text. It feels like the natural end of a thought that just happens to be incomplete. The reader swipes because the sentence demands resolution, not because a signpost told them to.

### 2. Each Slide Is a Breath, Not a Chapter
Slides are SHORT. 20–50 words. They feel like one exhale of a story being told out loud. When you read them, you hear a voice — pausing at line breaks, speeding through short fragments, landing on the final hook.

### 3. The Rhythm: Short. Short. Medium. Short.
Two punchy fragments. One slightly longer line. Snap back short. This creates forward momentum. Example:
"Rain, wind, and passing traffic.
He was out there.
Learning what sells, what doesn't, and how it feels when nobody buys.
Four years pass by..."

### 4. Line Breaks Are Dramatic Pauses
Line breaks don't follow grammar rules — they follow SPEECH rhythm. Break where you'd pause if telling this story out loud. Break before a reveal. Break to isolate a number. Break to let a contrast land.

### 5. Contrast Creates the Story
Every great Founded story has a gap: where they started vs. where they ended up. The wider the gap, the better the story. Surface it in Slide 1, echo it throughout, pay it off in the final slide.
- "$2 room at the YMCA" → "$13 billion empire"
- "Digging holes in roads" → "$1.2 billion sale"
- "Laid off from factory job" → "$975M exit"

### 6. Numbers Are Proof
Numbers signal truth. They stop the scroll. Always use digits. Always use the most specific number available. "$214M" not "over $200 million". "130 people" not "over a hundred". "£4,000-a-term" not "expensive school fees".

### 7. The Pivot Line
Almost every story has a variation of: "For most, that would be enough. Not for [Name]." This is the Founded signature move — it creates contrast between the ordinary response and the founder's extraordinary one. Use it once per story, never more.

### 8. The Callback Close
The final slide ties back to the opening or the origin. It creates a satisfying loop:
- Opens with "digging holes" → Closes with "Not bad for a guy who started out digging holes in roads."
- Opens with "$2 room at YMCA" → Closes with "From a $2 room at the YMCA to a multibillion-dollar empire."
- Opens with "laid off" → Closes with "From laid off… to near $1B… in six years."

### 9. Minimum Viable Slide Count
Use as few slides as the story demands. The goal is the shortest version that preserves the full narrative arc — not the most comfortable length. If 9 slides tell the complete story, use 9. Never add a slide to fill space or ease a transition. Every slide must be irreplaceable. When in doubt, cut.

### 10. The "Then" Turn
Many stories have a pivotal "Then" slide that shifts the entire trajectory. It usually comes after the initial success and signals that the REAL story is about to happen:
- "Then Shahid did the thing dishwashers aren't supposed to do…"
- "Then COVID hit."
- "Then Red Lobster came calling."

## GOLD-STANDARD EXAMPLE 1: JULIAN HEARN / HUEL
Slides: 11 | Hook type: Journey Arc + Transformation

Slide 1: Meet the founder who went from digging up roads to selling his company for $1.2b

Slide 2: This story doesn't start in a boardroom. It starts with a 16-year-old kid with dyslexia, who left school without any real qualifications. For most, that would define the rest of their lives. Not Julian Hearn.

Slide 3: He started out with a job digging holes in the road. It was there he learned the meaning of long hours and hard work. Then came the line that would change his life forever...

Slide 4: You're "too bright" to be digging holes. He went back to education. Then into marketing, where he could make mistakes and learn whilst on payroll.

Slide 5: Later in 2011, he builds and sells his own creative agency. For most, a win like that is enough to retire. Not for Hearn…

Slide 6: After not having a lunch break in four years, Julian began to notice others were skipping their lunch too... Offices full of people skipping meals, rushing through days, running on nothing.

Slide 7: So together with former NHS dietitian James Collier, he decides to fix it. Creating a convenient, nutritionally complete meal replacement built for speed. They called it Huel. Short for human fuel.

Slide 8: Steven Bartlett backs them early, investing in Huel's vision and joining the board. Idris Elba and his wife also back them. Soon Huel explodes from online-only to supermarket shelves across the UK, US and Europe.

Slide 9: By 2024, sales were at £214M, with pre-tax profit reaching £13.8M. The perfect recipe for global giant Danone, who just bought the business for €1B ($1.2b)

Slide 10: And with it, Hearn is reportedly set to make £400M (~$500m) from the deal.

Slide 11: Not bad for a guy who started out digging holes in roads.

## GOLD-STANDARD EXAMPLE 3: SHAHID KHAN / FLEX-N-GATE

Slide 10: Then Shahid did the thing dishwashers aren't supposed to do...

Slide 12: Fulham FC followed in 2013, then All Elite Wrestling with his son Tony in 2019.

Slide 13: Forbes put him on their cover and called him "The Face of The American Dream." From a $2 room at the YMCA to a $13 billion empire.

Slide 14: From a $2 room at the YMCA to a multibillion-dollar empire. Not bad for a kid who started out washing plates.

## SENTENCE ARCHITECTURE — THE MOST IMPORTANT RULE

The single biggest difference between correct and incorrect output is sentence length. The correct outputs are built from 3–8 word sentences. Most slides contain fragments. This is deliberate.

**DEFAULT SENTENCE LENGTH: 3–8 words.**
If your sentence does two jobs, split it into two sentences. If it does three, split it into three.

- RIGHT: "Long hours. Small pay. No shortcuts."
- WRONG: "He worked long hours for small pay with no shortcuts in sight."

- RIGHT: "Crowds boo him out of buildings. / For most, that'd be the end. / Not for Johnson…"
- WRONG: "Despite getting booed by crowds in his early WWE career, he persisted and eventually became one of the most famous entertainers in the world."

Every line is a beat. Write it like you're telling the story out loud and pausing between each sentence.

---

## PRESENT TENSE IS THE DEFAULT FOR PAST EVENTS

Tell past events in present tense. It creates immediacy. It pulls the reader into the scene.

- RIGHT: "He lands back at his parents' apartment in Tampa, sinks into depression, stares at a future with no plan."
- WRONG: "He landed back at his parents' apartment in Tampa, sank into depression, and stared at a future with no plan."

- RIGHT: "Crowds boo him out of buildings."
- WRONG: "Crowds booed him out of buildings."

Apply this across all non-reflective slides. Only switch to past tense for reflective closes or when the current moment (today/now) is contrasted with the past.

---

## PIVOT PLACEMENT — PRECISION RULE

The pivot ("For most, that'd be the end. Not for [Name]…") marks CHARACTER REVELATION — who this person is when things get hard.

**Place it at the FIRST MAJOR SETBACK OVERCOME — not at peak achievement.**

CORRECT: Johnson is booed out of WWE arenas in his first year. The pivot goes here. "For most, that'd be the end. Not for Johnson…"
WRONG: Johnson has already become The Rock with $100M earnings. The pivot goes here. "For most, that would be the whole story." — Too late. The reader already knows he won. The tension is gone.

Ask: at what moment would a normal person give up? Place the pivot there. That's when it reveals character.

---

## NUMBERS IN SLIDES — HOLD THEM BACK

The title handles the biggest number. Slides 2–8 earn the right to reveal it through human story. Slides 9–10 deliver it.

Do NOT front-load slides with financial figures. The journey is what makes the number meaningful.

Save the hard numbers for the payoff phase. Let the person carry the early slides.

---

## CELEBRITY VALIDATION — OMIT IT

Do not name-drop celebrities who used or endorsed the product to validate the story. The story is about the founder's journey. Celebrity validation dilutes the founder's own achievement.

WRONG (Tatcha): "Meghan Markle used it. Selena Gomez. Jennifer Aniston."
RIGHT (Tatcha): No celebrity names. The journey from broken skin to $500M sells itself.

**Exception:** When the celebrity IS the anchor detail in the title and narrative (Jay-Z in Ronnie Fieg's story — he's in the title, the origin scene, and the credibility anchor). The celebrity is part of the story's architecture, not dropped in as social proof.

---

## COMPANY AND INSTITUTION NAMES — DESCRIBE FIRST

Describe what a company or institution IS before (or instead of) naming it.

- WRONG: "She joined Accel Partners as a partner." → RIGHT: "She joined one of Silicon Valley's most powerful investment firms."
- WRONG: "He co-founded Aspect Ventures." → RIGHT: "She built her own investment firm."

Name only what the general audience will instantly recognise (Apple, Google, McDonald's, YouTube, WWE). Everything else — describe it.

---

## THE OBSERVATIONAL DEVICE

When the founder is in a humble setting (a job, a shop, an apartment), show their active mind:

"But he was watching. Learning. Waiting for his moment…"
"He's not just working. He's studying."
"Studying what people buy, what they ignore, what makes them come back…"

This creates a sense of inevitability — of course this person succeeded — and builds suspense for when the insight arrives.

---

## THE EMOTIONAL ANCHOR LINE

Every story should contain one line so crystallised it could stand alone. Not always a fact — often an image or a reframe.

"This club was family history with floodlights…"
"with a twist too poetic to script, her daughter was born the very same day she launched Tatcha"
"He didn't know it then, but that moment would define the rest of his life."

When you find the emotional anchor, protect it. Give it a line of its own. Don't bury it between facts.

---

## BUILDING MOMENTUM THROUGH COMPRESSION

Use short parallel lines to convey scale or sequence without slowing down:

"New York to Miami. / Los Angeles to Tokyo…"
"Nike. Adidas. Coca-Cola. Timberland."
"Film after film, Johnson isn't just the star, he's the producer with a share of every dollar."

These create a drumbeat — each item lands fast and the cumulative effect tells the story of scale.

## COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID

1. **Bolted-on hooks**: "What happens next will surprise you..." or "Stay tuned..." — these kill the flow. The hook must be organic.
2. **Telling the reader how to feel**: "This is incredible." "What an amazing journey." — let the facts do the emotional work.
3. **Over-explaining**: "Huel, which stands for Human Fuel, is a nutritionally complete meal replacement product..." — too much explanation kills momentum. Keep it punchy.
4. **Generic descriptors**: "successful entrepreneur", "innovative company", "game-changing product" — these mean nothing. Use specific facts instead.
5. **Padding to a comfortable length**: Every slide must be irreplaceable. If removing it wouldn't cost the story anything, it shouldn't be there. Always cut rather than pad.
6. **Ignoring the callback**: The final slide MUST connect back to the beginning or the origin story. It's the signature move.
7. **Flat Slide 2**: If Slide 2 doesn't make the reader physically unable to stop, the whole story fails. It's the most important slide after the title.
8. **Missing the "Then" turn**: The best stories have a moment where the trajectory shifts — "Then COVID hit." "Then Shahid did the thing dishwashers aren't supposed to do…" Look for this beat in every story.
9. **Treating temporal signposts as hooks**: "Fast-forward to 2024…" is a transition device, not a hook. A hook is: "For most, that'd be enough. Not for [Name]…"
10. **Same energy on every slide**: Vary the emotional register. Some slides should be punchy and fast. Some should breathe. Some should be just one devastating line.
11. **Insider references that exclude the reader**: "Sand Hill Road", "magna cum laude", "Series A", "term sheet", "SoMa" — any word or phrase that requires specialist knowledge to understand breaks the spell. The reader should always feel inside the story, never outside it. Replace every insider reference with plain English: "Sand Hill Road" → "where Silicon Valley's biggest investors work", "magna cum laude" → "top of his class", "Series A" → "first major funding round". If you can't translate it simply, cut it.
12. **Sentences that are too long**: If a sentence runs past 8 words, it almost certainly needs to be split. Two short sentences hit harder than one long one.
13. **Past tense when present tense would create more drama**: "He landed back in Tampa" is flat. "He lands back in Tampa" pulls the reader into the scene. Default to present tense for all past events.
14. **Pivot placed at peak achievement instead of at setback**: The pivot marks who this person is when things get hard — not when things are going well. Place it at the first major setback overcome, not at the moment of greatest success.
15. **Celebrity name-dropping as social proof**: Naming celebrities who used the product to validate the story dilutes the founder's own achievement. The founder's journey is the story.
16. **Naming companies and institutions the audience won't know**: "She joined Accel Partners" means nothing. "She joined one of Silicon Valley's most powerful investment firms" puts everyone inside the story. Describe what something IS before naming it — or instead of naming it.
17. **Front-loading numbers into early slides**: The title establishes the big number. Slides 2–8 earn the right to deliver it through human story. Slides 9–10 pay it off.

## THE THESIS CLOSE — END ON MEANING, NOT MONEY

The final slide (before the CTA) should land as an idea, not a statistic or a witty detail.

WRONG: "From $7 in a wallet to a $4.6B production company." — summary of facts, nothing to carry
RIGHT: "He didn't just learn retail. He rewrote it." — thesis about who he became

WRONG: "She built a $500M company from a garage." — fact
RIGHT: "She didn't set out to build a $500 million company. She just set out to solve the pain she was living in every single day." — meaning

Test: does the final slide give the reader something to carry away? A thesis close creates a takeaway. A facts close closes the browser tab.

Escalating superlative variant: "Not just America's first female billionaire investor. The first in the history of the industry." — the second sentence expands the scope of the achievement. The reader re-reads it and the number gets bigger.

---

## THE CHRONOLOGICAL BUILD — NEVER LEAD WITH THE ENDING

Start where the story starts. Build forward. Let the payoff arrive when it's earned.

WRONG: Open with the peak moment, then rewind: "In 2025, King Charles invited him to the Royal Box for his 90th birthday. But let's rewind to 1956…"
RIGHT: Open in 1956. Build through bus work, corner shop, wholesale, expansion, knighthood. The Royal Ascot moment arrives as the earned payoff.

Why: The reader has no reason to invest in the journey if they already know the destination.

Exception: Two-sentence Slide 1 for pure contrast stories — "She sold her engagement ring to start a skincare brand. / Now it's worth $500 million." This establishes the gap without revealing the full story. The HOW remains unknown.

---

## ESCALATION THROUGH REPETITION

When something happens multiple times in sequence and the accumulation is the point, repeat the beat rather than listing or summarising.

WRONG: "The limited-edition shoes sold out on multiple occasions between 2008 and 2011."
RIGHT: "The shoes sell out. / Then another pair. / Then another."

Each "then another" lands faster than the one before. The reader feels the acceleration. A list or summary cannot do this.

---

## CONTRADICTION FRAMING — DESCRIBE THE IMPOSSIBILITY, NOT THE ACHIEVEMENT

When writing a breakthrough slide, don't just name the achievement. Name the gap between who they were and what they won.

WRONG: "They landed Nike, Google, Amazon, Spotify, and Disney+."
RIGHT: "They landed Nike, Google, Amazon, Spotify, and Disney+. / Brands a small UK agency had no business pitching to."

"Had no business pitching to" makes the achievement feel impossible. The brand names alone are a list. The contradiction makes those names hit.

How to apply: After naming the achievement, add a second line naming who should never have been able to win it. "A 23-year-old who had never managed a restaurant." "A two-man team from a bedroom in Brighton." "A company that didn't exist three years earlier."

---

## THE COMPANY NAME AS A STANDALONE REVEAL

After the aha moment and the founding decision, hold the company name for the final beat of the slide — alone on its own line.

WRONG: "They saw a gap in what we now call creator marketing. The moment they landed back in the UK, they registered the idea and called it Whalar."
RIGHT: "They saw a gap in what we now call creator marketing. / The moment they landed back in the UK, they registered the idea.
Whalar."

The name drops as a full stop on the idea. The reader absorbs the setup first. The name lands with weight.

When to use: At the founding/naming slide, when the company name is being introduced for the first time. Don't use if the name has already appeared earlier.

---

## INDUSTRY-AS-CHASER FRAMING

At the acquisition or major validation beat, frame the event as the market arriving at the founder — not the founder arriving at the market.

WRONG: "They accepted an acquisition offer from one of the world's biggest consulting firms."
RIGHT: "And the industry had finally caught up."

The first positions them as sellers. The second positions them as pioneers who were ahead of their time. Use this line (or a variation) immediately before naming the acquirer — it sets the tone before the fact lands.

---

## FRAGMENT ESCALATION — THE SECOND PUNCH

After a strong factual claim, isolate the follow-on detail as its own fragment:

WRONG: "They were named Agency of the Year three consecutive times."
RIGHT: "Soon they were named Agency of the Year. Three years running."

"Three years running." as a standalone fragment lands harder than a clause. The isolation signals that this detail is too important to share a sentence.

Apply to: streaks, records, unexpected repeats. "Twice." "Back to back." "Before he turned 25."

---

## "THIS WEEK" — URGENCY MARKER FOR CURRENT-EVENT STORIES

When a story culminates in a recent deal or announcement, open the slide with a time marker before the entity name:

WRONG: "Accenture acquired Whalar's agency arm."
RIGHT: "This week, Accenture — one of the world's largest consulting firms, $70B in annual revenue — acquired Whalar's agency arm."

"This week" makes the story feel live. The reader is in the moment, not reading history.

Inline appositive: When introducing an unfamiliar company, explain it in the same breath: "Accenture, one of the world's largest consulting firms, $70B in annual revenue." No separate slide needed — the credential lands without pausing the story.
